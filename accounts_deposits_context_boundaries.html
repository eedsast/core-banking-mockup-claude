Accounts vs Deposits — Context Boundaries

Domain analysis for: Current accounts · Savings · Term deposits · Overdraft

✓
Split into two bounded contexts. With current accounts + overdraft on one side and term deposits on the other, your business rules diverge enough to justify separation. Current accounts are operational — daily transactions, overdraft limits, real-time balance checks. Deposits are contractual — maturity dates, interest accrual, rollover rules, early withdrawal penalties. Forcing these into one service means one service owns two fundamentally different lifecycle models, two different interest engines, and two different regulatory reporting paths. That is too much for one bounded context.

Shared concepts — how the two services interact
Deposit funding link
When a deposit is placed, Deposits service instructs Accounts service to debit the funding account. Accounts service posts the debit and fires a confirmation event. Both services stay consistent via events — never direct DB calls.
Maturity sweep
On maturity, Deposits service fires a maturity.completed event. Accounts service receives it and credits the proceeds to the customer's current account. No synchronous call between services.
Customer service
Both services link to the same customer_id from the Customer / CRM service. Neither owns customer data — they consume it. KYC status checked via Customer service before account or deposit opening.
Currency & rates
Both consume enabled currencies from Administration service. Deposit interest rates come from the Deposits service own rate config. FX revaluation for FX deposits handled within Deposits service, GL event fired to Accounting.

Anti-pattern to avoid: Do not create a generic "Account service" that tries to handle all account types through a discriminator column or polymorphic table. A table with 40 nullable columns where 20 are only relevant to term deposits and 20 only to current accounts is a design smell. The domain models are different enough that they deserve separate tables, separate APIs, separate deployment cycles, and separate teams owning them. The short-term convenience of one service becomes long-term pain when the deposit interest engine needs to change independently of the overdraft limit logic.

Why split is the right answer here

Business rules diverge completely — lifecycle, interest engine, rollover, early exit are all different
Transaction frequency differs — Accounts service has high-frequency real-time posting; Deposits has rare lifecycle events
Regulatory reporting paths are different — current account reporting vs liquidity maturity ladder
Teams can evolve independently — deposit rate engine changes do not risk current account posting
Scaling profiles differ — Accounts service needs horizontal scaling for payment throughput; Deposits is batch-oriented

Rules for keeping the split clean

Never share a database — each service owns its own schema and tables entirely
Deposits instructs Accounts service via events — never via direct API call or shared table
Both post to Accounting via the same gl.journal.requested event — Accounting does not know or care who sent it
customer_id is the only shared key — neither service duplicates customer profile data
Overdraft lives in Accounts service — it is a feature of a current account, not a separate service
