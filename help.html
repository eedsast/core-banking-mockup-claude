Loan Management
Loan Products

A Loan Product is the master template that defines the financial and operational parameters for a Loan products. Every loan account opened on the platform is bound to exactly one Loan Product, and inherits its rules at origination.

Key parameters defined at the product level include the allowed Currencies, Interest rate structure, Repayment schedule type, Interest calculation method, tenor limits, fee structure, and accounting treatment. Individual loan accounts may override some parameters within bounds set by the product.

It is also common to segment loans as per Client categories, each class typically has its own risk model:

  • SME (Small & Medium Enterprises): Loans to smaller businesses, often grouped by industry

  • Corporate: Large, idiosyncratic loans that require individual assessment rather than just statistical modeling

  • Interbank: Loans and placements with other financial institutions.

Additionally, it can also be used for segmentation on current (short-term ≤ 12 months) and non-current (long-term > 12 months) asset requested under Under IAS 1 Presentation of Financial statements.

Accounting Method

Accrual or Cash basis. The Accounting Method defines how interest is recognised and posted to the General Ledger throughout the life of a loan. It determines the timing and amount of each accrual entry.

Available Methods

Method Description Typical use
Accrual Interest is accrued daily and posted to an accrued interest receivable account. Revenue is recognised as it is earned, regardless of cash receipt. Standard retail and SME lending.
Cash Basis Interest is recognised only when cash is received. No accrual entries are posted in advance. Applied in some countries or for written-off loans.
Important Once a loan account is opened, the Accounting Method cannot be changed. Changes to the method on a Loan Product apply only to accounts opened after the change is saved.

How Accrual Works — Straight-line amortization

Each night the EoD Interest Accrual batch calculates the interest accrued for the day using the formula below, then posts a debit to Accrued Interest Receivable and a credit to Interest Income. Posting will be done according to the Posting frequency parameter, either daily or monthly (cumulative amount at the end of month).

Daily accrual = Outstanding principal × Annual rate ÷ Day count basis -- Example (Actual/Actual, 10% p.a., balance 10 000): -- Daily accrual = 10 000 × 0.10 / 365 (or 366) = 2.7397 or 2.7322

Loan Management › Loan Products
Posting frequency
Possible values: Daily · Monthly

Defines how and when interest accrual entries are recognised in the General Ledger. Under Accrual accounting, accrued interest can be posted to the GL either every day, or once at month-end as a cumulative amount covering all days in the period.

Daily posting produces a higher transaction volume but makes intra-month reporting more accurate. Monthly posting is lighter on the GL but means the trial balance does not reflect accrued interest until the month-end close.

Loan Management › Loan Products
EIR method amortization

Possibe values: Boolean flag — True / False.
If marked True, EIR method amortization will be enabled.

For clients that report according to IFRS 9 (usually mandatory for financial institutions with a banking licence, but not for EMIs), double General Ledger booking will be enabled. Double GL keeping is mandatory because we still need straight-line amortization for clients, and because the tax authorities do not recognise IFRS 9 impairment (ECL) as a tax-deductible expense until the loss is actually realised or documented.

Under the EIR method the system calculates an internal rate of return (effective interest rate) that equates the present value of all expected cash flows (including fees) to the initial loan amount. This rate is then applied to the amortized cost balance each period to determine income. See Effective Interest Rate (EIR) for the full calculation.

EIR (Effective Interest Rate) Interest and fee income are spread over the loan life using the constant-yield (effective interest rate) method. Origination fees and direct costs are included in the EIR calculation. IFRS 9 / IFRS 15 compliant recognition; mortgage and structured products.
Regulatory note IFRS 9 requires the EIR method for loans measured at amortised cost. Using Cash basis or simple Accrual basis accounting for performing loans may result in under-recognition of income and require restatement.

Product Currencies

This is list of currencies selected from Organisation level that is subset of standard ISO 4217 currency codes (e.g. EUR, USD, CHF, ALL) in which loans on this product may be denominated.

Interest Type

Determines whether the interest rate applied to a loan is fixed throughout its life or can vary in line with a reference rate.

Type Description
Fixed The interest rate is set at origination and does not change for the loan term. Provides payment certainty for the borrower.
Variable NOT YET IMPLEMENTED — The rate floats against a reference index (e.g. EURIBOR, LIBOR, central bank rate). Repricing occurs on a defined schedule.
Mixed NOT YET IMPLEMENTED — Fixed for an initial period (e.g. 24 months), then converts to variable for the remainder of the term. The initial Repayment Plan is calculated for the full period using the initial interest rate, because the future rate is not yet known.

Loan Management › Loan Products
Day Count Convention
Possible values: THIRTY_360 · ACT_360 · ACT_365 · ACT_ACT

Parameter DayCountConvention defined at Loan Product level. Defines how the number of days in an interest period and the number of days in a year are calculated. Different conventions produce different interest amounts even with the same nominal rate. Must be applied consistently across Loan Product, Loan Simulate, Loan Generate, and Loan Open APIs.

Value Days in period Days in year Description
THIRTY_360 Each month treated as 30 days 360 Every month treated as 30 days, year as 360. Calendar-independent. EU consumer credit standard. Cleanest math for monthly annuity loans. We use the European variant where every month (including February) is treated as 30 days.
ACT_360 Actual calendar days 360 (fixed) Actual calendar days between dates, divided by 360-day year. Used for money market loans, EURIBOR-linked products. Overcharges customer by ~1.39% effective vs headline rate.
ACT_365 Actual calendar days 365 (fixed) Actual calendar days, divided by 365-day year (fixed, ignores leap years). UK retail standard.
ACT_ACT Actual calendar days 365 or 366 (leap year) Actual calendar days, divided by actual year length (365 or 366). Splits at year boundary for periods crossing 31-Dec. Most precise — used for bonds and ISDA-governed instruments.

Loan Management › Loan Products
Repayment Schedule pattern
Possible values: EQUAL_INSTALMENT · EQUAL_PRINCIPAL · FLAT_RATE · BULLET · BALLOON · MULTI_DISBURSEMENT

Parameter RepaymentSchedulePattern defined at Loan Product level. Defines how the outstanding principal and interest are distributed across instalments over the loan term.

Value Description
EQUAL_INSTALMENT Annuity loan (a.k.a. French method). Total instalment is fixed; interest portion decreases and principal portion increases over time. Most common consumer/SME loan structure.
EQUAL_PRINCIPAL Linear loan (a.k.a. German method). Principal portion is fixed; interest portion decreases over time, so total instalment decreases. Common for corporate and infrastructure loans.
FLAT_RATE Flat-rate loan. Equal principal + equal interest = equal total instalment, with interest calculated on original principal. Calculated as principal ÷ number of instalments and total interest ÷ number of instalments. Effective APR ~2× the headline rate. Restricted for EU consumer use; viable for corporate/microfinance.
BULLET Principal repaid in full at maturity; only interest paid periodically during the term. Used for short-term corporate facilities and bridge financing.
BALLOON Post-MVP. Regular instalments plus a larger final instalment that repays remaining principal.
MULTI_DISBURSEMENT Post-MVP. Principal disbursed in multiple tranches; schedule recalculated as each tranche is drawn.

Repayment Date pattern
Possible values: REGULAR_ANCHORED_TO_DISBURSEMENT · IRREGULAR_EXPLICIT_DATE · CUSTOM_SCHEDULE

Parameter RepaymentDatePattern defined at Loan Product level (default) and overridable on the Loan Simulation screen. Controls whether the schedule of due dates is auto-generated from the disbursement date or requires manual specification of the first repayment date.

Value Description
REGULAR_ANCHORED_TO_DISBURSEMENT First Repayment Date is auto-computed from Disbursement Date plus the Interest Calculation frequency. Subsequent dates follow the same frequency. The First Repayment Date field is pre-filled and non-editable on the Loan Simulation screen.
IRREGULAR_EXPLICIT_DATE User must manually specify the First Repayment Date on the Loan Simulation screen. Used for irregular first periods (e.g. disburse mid-month, first repayment on month-end). Activates the IrregularPeriodAdjustment parameter.
CUSTOM_SCHEDULE Post-MVP. All instalment dates explicitly defined by the user — used for project finance and corporate facilities with custom drawdown / repayment patterns.

Loan Management › Loan Products
Irregular Period Adjustment
Possible values: TRUE_EQUAL_INSTALMENT · ADJUST_LAST_INSTALMENT · ADJUST_FIRST_INSTALMENT

Parameter IrregularPeriodAdjustment defined at Loan Product level. Applied only when RepaymentDatePattern = IRREGULAR_EXPLICIT_DATE. Determines how the broken-period interest arising from a non-standard first period is absorbed into the repayment schedule.

Value Description
TRUE_EQUAL_INSTALMENT The annuity is recalculated so that all instalments are mathematically equal despite the irregular first period. Customer pays exactly the same amount every period.
ADJUST_LAST_INSTALMENT Standard annuity calculated on regular periods; the last instalment absorbs the irregularity (slightly higher or lower than other instalments).
ADJUST_FIRST_INSTALMENT Standard annuity calculated on regular periods; the first instalment carries the broken-period interest (typically higher than subsequent instalments).

Loan Management › Loan Products
Interest Calculation Method
Possible values: CONFORMING · PROPORTIONAL

Parameter InterestCalculationMethod defined at Loan Product level. Determines how the annual interest rate is converted into the periodic rate used for each instalment's interest computation. Both methods compound — they differ in how the conversion is performed.

Value Description
CONFORMING Periodic rate computed as (1 + r)1/n − 1. The effective annual rate equals the stated annual rate exactly. Mandatory for EU consumer loans (HNB EKS disclosure requirement). Aligns with the Croatian regulatory term "konformna metoda".
PROPORTIONAL Periodic rate computed as r / n (simple division). Effective annual rate exceeds the stated rate due to compounding (e.g. 17% nominal → 18.39% effective). Allowed for corporate / wholesale facilities; not allowed for EU consumer products. Aligns with the Croatian regulatory term "proporcionalna metoda".
Worked example at 17 % p.a., monthly compounding Conforming: rm = (1.17)1/12 − 1 = 1.317 % → effective annual rate 17.000 %.
Proportional: rm = 17 % ÷ 12 = 1.4167 % → effective annual rate 18.389 % (a hidden uplift of ~139 bps).

Repayment Plan — Worked Examples

The four schedules below are all generated from the same loan parameters, so the differences come purely from the chosen Repayment Schedule pattern. All amounts are in EUR.

Common input parameters Principal: 1 000.00 EUR
Term: 6 monthly instalments
Nominal interest rate: 17 % p.a.
Disbursement date: 30 November 2025
Day count convention: 30 / 360 (monthly rate = 17 % ÷ 12 = 1.41667 %)
First due date: 30 December 2025

1) Equal instalment

The total monthly instalment is constant. Each payment is split between a growing principal component and a shrinking interest component. The annuity amount is calculated as:

EMI/PMT = P × r × (1+r)n / ((1+r)n – 1) = 1 000 × 0.0141667 × 1.088068 / 0.088068 = 175.03 EUR / month

Loan Management › Loan Products
Repayment Rounding mode
Possible values: Round to nearest · Round up

Defines the rounding applied to each scheduled instalment amount during plan generation.

  • Round to nearest uses standard half-up rounding to the minor currency unit (e.g. 2 decimals for EUR). Residual rounding differences are absorbed in the last instalment if Adjust last instalment is enabled.
  • Round up always rounds the instalment amount up to the next minor unit. Favours the lender and guarantees that the schedule fully amortises principal and interest.

Loan Management › Loan Products
Short Month Handling
Possible values: PAYMENT_DATE · LAST_DAY_IN_MONTH

Parameter ShortMonthHandling defined at Loan Product level. Controls how due dates are resolved when the anchor day from the disbursement date does not exist in a target month — for example, when a loan disbursed on the 31st reaches February, which has only 28 or 29 days.

Value Description
PAYMENT_DATE The instalment for the short month falls on the closest available calendar day (e.g. Feb 28 / Feb 29). In subsequent longer months the schedule reverts back to the original anchor day. Example: loan disbursed 31-Jan → Feb 28 → Mar 31 → Apr 30 → May 31.
LAST_DAY_IN_MONTH Once the schedule encounters a short month, it locks permanently to the last day of every subsequent month regardless of how long they are. Example: loan disbursed 31-Jan → Feb 28 → Mar 31 → Apr 30 → May 31 (visually identical here but the logic differs for partial periods and end-of-month-anchored loans starting on Jan 30 or Feb 27).

Interest calculated at
Beginning of period · End of period

Specifies whether interest is charged in advance or in arrears.

  • Beginning of period (in advance) — interest for the period is calculated and recognised on the period start date, on the opening balance. Common for discount loans and some commercial facilities.
  • End of period (in arrears) — interest is calculated and recognised on the period end date, after taking into account any movements during the period. The standard approach for retail loans.

Business Day Convention
Possible values: NEXT_BUSINESS_DAY · PREVIOUS_BUSINESS_DAY · NONE

Parameter BusinessDayConvention defined at Loan Product level (formerly NonWorkingDayAdjustment). Controls what happens when a generated due date falls on a weekend or a public holiday in the loan's calendar. Orthogonal to ShortMonthHandling.

Grace period
Yes · No

Boolean. If Yes, additional parameters below have to be defined.

Grace period days

The Grace Period refers to a contractually defined number of days following the scheduled due date of a loan instalment during which the payment can be made without incurring late fees or penalties.

Interest calculated during grace

Yes or No. No interest is charged on the instalment amount if the full payment is received within the defined grace period (e.g. 5 days).

  • If Yes and payment is not received by the end of the grace period, penalty interest is calculated and applied from the original due date.
  • If No and payment is not received by the end of the grace period, penalty interest is calculated and applied from the expiry date of the grace period.

Loan Management › Loan Products
Adjust last instalment
Yes · No

Controls how rounding residuals introduced during schedule generation are handled.

  • Yes — any rounding difference between the sum of generated instalments and the contractual total is absorbed entirely into the final instalment, so the closing balance after the last payment is exactly zero. This is the standard approach.
  • No — the residual is distributed evenly across all instalments. All instalments may differ slightly from the headline calculated amount.

Loan Management
Collection Definitions

A Collection Definition specifies the rules for triggering and processing collections when a loan instalment becomes overdue.

Collection Trigger

Collections can be triggered automatically by the EoD batch when a due date passes without full payment, or manually by an authorised user. The trigger threshold (number of days past due) is set per product.

Collection Allocation

When a collection payment is received, the platform allocates it in the following default order: Penalty interest → Overdue interest → Overdue principal → Current interest → Current principal. The allocation order can be customised per product.

Loan Management
Penalty Definitions

Penalty Definitions control the rate and basis of charges applied when a borrower misses a scheduled payment or repays early, as well as any standing fees attached to the product.

Common Penalty / Fee Types

Penalty Type Description
Late Payment Penalty A daily rate applied to the overdue amount for each calendar day past the due date. Expressed as an annual percentage rate.
Early Repayment Fee A one-time charge calculated on the prepaid principal, compensating the bank for lost future interest income. May be a flat amount or a percentage.
Non-utilisation Fee Applied to revolving and overdraft facilities where the borrower does not draw the committed limit. Charged on the undrawn portion.

Configuration Fields

Every Penalty Definition is described by the fields below. Some fields appear conditionally depending on the selected Calculation method.

Scope & Trigger

Scope
Loan Product · Loan Account

Defines the level at which the penalty definition applies. Loan Product scope means the penalty is inherited by every loan account opened under the product. Loan Account scope means the penalty is attached only to a specific account (e.g. as part of a restructure or bespoke contract).

Trigger event
Up Front · Per Instalment · Periodic · Event Based

Determines when the penalty or fee is generated by the system.

  • Up Front — charged once at loan opening / disbursement (typical for processing fees, origination fees).
  • Per Instalment — charged together with every scheduled instalment (e.g. a monthly administration fee).
  • Periodic — charged at a fixed time interval independent of instalments (e.g. annual maintenance fee on a revolving facility).
  • Event Based — triggered by a specific business event such as a missed payment, early repayment, restructure, or top-up.

Calculation

Calculation method
Percentage · Fixed

Drives how the charge amount is computed and which downstream fields are required.

  • Percentage — the charge is calculated as a percentage of a chosen base; the Calculation base, Rate (%), Min. amount and Max. amount fields become editable.
  • Fixed — the charge is a fixed monetary value; the Fixed amount and Currency fields become editable.
Calculation base (visible when Calculation method = Percentage)
Loan Amount · Outstanding Principal · Overdue Amount · Instalment Amount

The value the rate is applied to.

  • Loan Amount — the original disbursed principal. Used for up-front and one-off fees.
  • Outstanding Principal — the principal balance at the time the charge is triggered. Used for periodic fees and early repayment charges.
  • Overdue Amount — the overdue principal and/or interest at trigger time. Used for late-payment penalties.
  • Instalment Amount — the scheduled instalment amount. Used for per-instalment fees.
Rate (%) (visible when Calculation method = Percentage)

The percentage applied to the Calculation base. Entered as a number (e.g. 2.5 for 2.5 %). For periodic and per-instalment percentages, the value represents the rate for the relevant period; for an annualised penalty rate (e.g. late-payment interest) the system will pro-rate it by the day count basis.

Min. amount (optional, Percentage only)

Floor value for the calculated charge. If the percentage calculation yields less than this amount, the charge is rounded up to the Min. amount. Useful to make small percentages economically meaningful (e.g. "0.5 % of outstanding, but never less than 10 EUR").

Max. amount (optional, Percentage only)

Ceiling value for the calculated charge. If the percentage calculation yields more than this amount, the charge is capped at the Max. amount. Frequently required by consumer-protection regulations on early repayment fees.

Fixed amount (visible when Calculation method = Fixed)

The exact monetary amount charged when the trigger event fires, expressed in the selected Currency. Independent of the loan or instalment size.

Currency (visible when Calculation method = Fixed)

ISO 4217 currency of the Fixed amount (e.g. EUR, USD). If different from the loan currency, the system converts at the FX rate effective on the booking date when the charge is posted.

Included in EIR calculation
Yes · No

Determines whether the fee or penalty is part of the cash flows used to compute the loan's Effective Interest Rate.

  • Yes — typical for predictable, contractual charges known at origination (processing fee, commitment fee, periodic admin fee). IFRS 9 requires these to be amortised into interest income via the EIR.
  • No — typical for contingent or punitive charges that depend on borrower behaviour and cannot be reliably estimated at origination (late-payment penalty, early-repayment fee). These are recognised in income only when the event occurs.
Field visibility The form dynamically shows or hides fields based on the selected Calculation method. Percentage reveals Calculation base, Rate, Min. and Max. amount; Fixed reveals Fixed amount and Currency. The Scope, Trigger event and "Included in EIR calculation" fields are always visible.

A Loan Account is an instance of a Loan Product, created when a credit application is approved and funds are scheduled for disbursement. While the Loan Product defines the template (rates, conventions, patterns), the Loan Account holds the contractual specifics for one borrower: the principal, the disbursement date, the repayment schedule, the running balance, and all lifecycle-dependent attributes such as days in arrears and performance stage. Effective Interest Rate (EIR)

The Effective Interest Rate (EIR) is the rate that exactly discounts the estimated future cash flows of a loan back to its net carrying amount at initial recognition. Under IFRS 9 it is the basis for interest income recognition on amortised-cost assets.

What is included in the EIR

  • Nominal contractual interest
  • Origination fees charged to the borrower (e.g. processing fee, commitment fee)
  • Direct incremental origination costs (e.g. broker commissions)
  • Any discount or premium on the principal

Calculation Approach

The system solves for the internal rate of return r in the following equation, where CFt is the cash flow at time t and n is the number of periods:

Net proceeds = Σ [ CFt / (1 + r)t ] for t = 1 to n -- Net proceeds = Loan amount – upfront fees + direct costs -- CF = scheduled principal + interest payments
Preliminary vs Final EIR A preliminary EIR is calculated at application stage using the expected repayment schedule. The final EIR is locked at loan opening once all fees are confirmed and may differ slightly from the preliminary figure. Both are visible in the loan account detail view.

Days in Arrears
Type: Integer · Read-only · Updated by End-of-Day batch

Parameter DaysInArrears (DiA) defined at Loan Account level. The number of calendar days the oldest unpaid scheduled instalment has been overdue as of the current business date. Calculated as the difference between today and the due date of the earliest unsettled instalment. If the customer is fully paid up, the value is zero.

DiA is the most important behavioural metric on a loan account: it drives the IFRS 9 staging decision, the asset risk classification under HNB rules, penalty interest accrual, collection workflow triggers, and regulatory reporting (ANACREDIT, FINREP, COREP, NPL flag). The counter resets to zero only when all overdue instalments are settled; partial payments may or may not reduce DiA depending on the configured payment-allocation hierarchy.

Max Days in Arrears (Lifecycle)
Type: Integer · Read-only · Monotonically non-decreasing

Parameter MaxDaysInArrearsLifecycle defined at Loan Account level. The highest value of DaysInArrears ever observed on this loan account since origination. Once a higher DiA value is reached this attribute is updated and never decreases, even after the customer cures their overdue position.

This is a long-memory indicator used for behavioural scoring, internal credit risk modelling, SICR (Significant Increase in Credit Risk) triggers under IFRS 9, customer relationship management, and pricing decisions on future credit applications. A loan that briefly went 95 days past due before curing is still treated differently from a loan that never went above 5 days, even if both have DiA = 0 today.

Performance Stage

In the context of IFRS 9, "Performance Stages" refer to the three-stage model used to measure the credit risk of a loan. This determines how much money you must set aside as ECL — Expected Credit Loss (impairment) — and how you calculate interest income. The model tracks the journey of a loan from "healthy" to "defaulted".

Stage 1: Performing (healthy)

This is where every loan starts upon initial recognition.

  • Credit Risk: No significant increase in credit risk since the loan was issued.
  • Impairment (ECL): Standard loss on a portfolio basis that you expect from default events that are possible within the next 12 months. Usually calculated from three components: PD (Probability of default), LGD (Loss given default), EAD (Exposure at default), defined at Loan Product level, as ECL = PD × LGD × EAD.
  • Interest Income: Calculated on the gross carrying amount.

Stage 2: Underperforming (Deteriorating)

The loan moves here if there is a Significant Increase in Credit Risk (SICR), but it has not defaulted yet. A common trigger is a payment being more than 30 days past due.

  • Credit Risk: Risk has increased significantly since day one.
  • Impairment (ECL): You must now calculate the Lifetime ECL. This is a much larger buffer because it covers the risk of default over the entire remaining life of the loan.
  • Interest Income: Still calculated on the gross carrying amount.

Stage 3: Non-performing (Impaired / Default)

The loan is considered "credit-impaired". This usually happens when a payment is more than 90 days past due, or the borrower is in bankruptcy.

  • Credit Risk: Objective evidence of impairment (default).
  • Impairment (ECL): You continue to use Lifetime ECL, but the probability of default is now essentially 100%.
  • Interest Income: Calculated on the net carrying amount (Balance minus the Impairment). This reduces the profit shown on the books.

The "Cure" (Moving Backwards) A loan can move back from Stage 2 to Stage 1, or from Stage 3 to Stage 2, but only after a "probation period". For example, if a borrower was 45 days late (Stage 2) but then pays everything and stays on time for 3–6 months, the bank can move them back to Stage 1.

Interest Calculation Formulas
Universal Formula Reference

Three universal formulas cover all four day-count conventions (30/360, ACT/360, ACT/365, ACT/ACT) and both calculation methods (Conforming, Proportional): the periodic rate, the EMI / annuity, and the per-period interest portion. Only the value fed into them changes; the formulas themselves are identical across every product configuration.

Symbol Meaning
P Principal (loan amount)
r Annual nominal interest rate (e.g. 0.17)
n Number of instalments
Bk-1 Outstanding balance at start of period k
Dk Days in period k (per day-count convention)
Yk Days in year for period k (per day-count convention)
fk Year-fraction (accrual factor) for period k = Dk / Yk
rm Periodic rate used to compute the annuity
A Equal instalment (EMI)
Ik Interest portion of instalment k

Interest Calculation Formulas
Periodic Rate (rm)

Converts the annual nominal rate into the periodic rate, according to the selected Interest Calculation Method.

CONFORMING: r_m = (1 + r)^f − 1 PROPORTIONAL: r_m = r × f where f is the year-fraction resolved by the day-count convention: 30/360 f = 30 / 360 = 1/12 ACT/360 f = ActualDays / 360 ACT/365 f = ActualDays / 365 ACT/ACT f = ActualDays / Y (Y = 365 or 366; split at year-end)
Annuity vs per-period fraction For the annuity calculation (formula 2), f is the regular-period fraction (the nominal month = 1/12 for monthly). For 30/360 this always equals 1/12; for ACT conventions most core banking systems also use the nominal 1/12 for the EMI base, then apply each period's actual day-count fraction for the interest split (formula 3).
↑ Back to top
Interest Calculation Formulas
EMI / Annuity

Computes the equal instalment. Identical for all four day-count conventions and both methods — only the value of rm (from formula 1) changes.

A = P × [ r_m × (1 + r_m)^n ] / [ (1 + r_m)^n − 1 ]
Edge case — zero rate If rm = 0 (interest-free loan) the formula divides by zero. Handle separately: A = P / n when rm = 0.
↑ Back to top
Interest Calculation Formulas
Interest Portion per Period

Splits each instalment into interest and principal. Uses the same method choice as formula 1, but with each period's own actual year-fraction fk = Dk / Yk.

I_k = B_(k-1) × g(r, f_k) CONFORMING: g(r, f_k) = (1 + r)^(f_k) − 1 PROPORTIONAL: g(r, f_k) = r × f_k then: P_k = A − I_k B_k = B_(k-1) − P_k
ACT/ACT year-boundary split When a period crosses 31-Dec, the year-fraction is the sum of two sub-fractions: fk = (Dk,Y1 / Y1) + (Dk,Y2 / Y2), where Y1, Y2 ∈ {365, 366} depending on whether each calendar year is a leap year.
Consistency rule The method choice (CONFORMING / PROPORTIONAL) must be identical in formula 1 and formula 3. Mixing them breaks the schedule — the last instalment will not close the balance to zero.
↑ Back to top
Interest Calculation Formulas
Last Instalment Rule (Balance Closing)

Because of rounding, the final instalment is computed differently to guarantee a clean zero balance.

For k < n: I_k = B_(k-1) × g(r, f_k) P_k = A − I_k For k = n (last): I_n = B_(n-1) × g(r, f_n) P_n = B_(n-1) # pay off whatever remains A_n = P_n + I_n # may differ from A by a cent or two

The last instalment An absorbs all accumulated rounding from previous periods.

Interest Calculation Formulas
Unified Pseudocode

One interest engine, parameterised by InterestCalculationMethod and DayCountConvention.

function buildSchedule(P, r, n, method, dayCount, dates): # Step 1 — periodic rate for annuity (nominal regular-period fraction) f_regular = regularFraction(dayCount) # 1/12 for monthly rm = periodicRate(method, r, f_regular) # Step 2 — annuity (EMI) if rm == 0: A = round(P / n, 2) else: A = round(P * (rm*(1+rm)^n) / ((1+rm)^n - 1), 2) # Step 3 — per-period interest split balance = P for k in 1..n: f_k = yearFraction(dayCount, dates[k-1], dates[k]) periodRate_k = applyMethod(method, r, f_k) interest = round(balance * periodRate_k, 2) if k == n: principal = round(balance, 2) # close the balance instalment = round(principal + interest, 2) else: principal = round(A - interest, 2) instalment = A balance = round(balance - principal, 2) emit(k, dates[k], interest, principal, instalment, balance) # ── helpers ────────────────────────────────────────────── function periodicRate(method, r, f): # == applyMethod if method == CONFORMING: return (1 + r)^f - 1 else: return r * f function yearFraction(dayCount, d0, d1): switch dayCount: case THIRTY_360: return 30 / 360 case ACT_360: return actualDays(d0, d1) / 360 case ACT_365: return actualDays(d0, d1) / 365 case ACT_ACT: if year(d0) == year(d1): Y = isLeap(year(d0)) ? 366 : 365 return actualDays(d0, d1) / Y else: # crosses year-end ye = endOfYear(d0) # Dec 31 Y1 = isLeap(year(d0)) ? 366 : 365 Y2 = isLeap(year(d1)) ? 366 : 365 return actualDays(d0, ye)/Y1 + actualDays(ye, d1)/Y2

Glossary — All Terms A–Z

Quick definitions for all key terms used across the platform.

Term Definition
Accrual The daily recognition of interest income earned but not yet received in cash.
Amortised Cost The amount at which a financial asset is measured after deducting principal repayments and adding/deducting cumulative amortisation using the EIR method.
Annuity A series of equal periodic payments combining principal and interest such that the loan is fully repaid at maturity.
Asset Risk Classification (ARC) Regulatory risk category assigned to a loan exposure per HNB classification rules (A, B-1, B-2, B-3, C). See Asset Risk Classification.
Balloon Payment A large lump-sum payment due at maturity representing the residual principal balance after regular instalment payments.
Bullet Loan A loan where only interest is paid periodically and the full principal is repaid at maturity.
Conforming Method Rate conversion method where the periodic rate is computed as (1+r)1/n − 1 so the effective annual rate equals the stated annual rate exactly. Mandatory for EU consumer loans (HNB EKS). Croatian term: konformna metoda. See Interest Calculation Method.
Day Count Convention The rule used to calculate the number of days in an interest period and the number of days in a year for interest accrual purposes. See Day Count Convention.
Days in Arrears (DiA) Number of calendar days the oldest unpaid instalment has been overdue. Drives staging, classification, penalty accrual, and collections. See Days in Arrears.
DTI Debt-to-Income ratio. Total monthly debt obligations divided by gross monthly income. Used as a credit underwriting criterion.
EAD Exposure at Default. The expected amount outstanding at the time of default. Component of ECL.
ECL Expected Credit Loss. Calculated as PD × LGD × EAD under IFRS 9.
EIR Effective Interest Rate. The rate that equates the present value of all expected cash flows to the net carrying amount at initial recognition. See EIR section.
EoD End of Day. The nightly batch processing window during which accruals, penalty calculations, balance updates, and reporting extracts are generated.
Flat Rate Loan A loan where principal and interest portions are equal in every instalment. Interest is calculated on the original principal (not declining balance), so the effective APR is roughly double the headline rate. Also called Add-On Interest Loan (US). Restricted for EU consumer use.
GL General Ledger. The master record of all financial transactions, organised by account.
IFRS 9 International Financial Reporting Standard 9. Governs the classification, measurement, and impairment of financial instruments.
LGD Loss Given Default. The percentage of EAD expected to be lost in the event of default, net of recoveries.
LTV Loan-to-Value ratio. Loan amount divided by the appraised value of collateral. Used as a credit criterion for secured products.
Non-performing Loan (NPL) A loan where the borrower is 90 or more days past due on a scheduled payment. Accrual is typically suspended on NPLs.
PD Probability of Default. Likelihood that the borrower will default within a given horizon (12 months or lifetime).
Period An accounting period, typically a calendar month, during which transactions are recorded and at the end of which the GL is closed.
Proportional Method Rate conversion method where the periodic rate is computed as r / n (simple division). The effective annual rate exceeds the stated rate due to compounding (e.g. 17% nominal → 18.39% effective). Allowed for corporate/wholesale; not allowed for EU consumer products. Croatian term: proporcionalna metoda. See Interest Calculation Method.
SICR Significant Increase in Credit Risk. The IFRS 9 trigger that moves a loan from Stage 1 to Stage 2.
Tenant An isolated organisational entity within the platform. Each tenant has its own data, configuration, and GL.
